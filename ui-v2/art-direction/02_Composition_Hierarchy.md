# 02 · Composition & Hierarchy

> **Tier:** Core · **Status:** Migrated (from `UI-new`, `Rendering-Lighting`)
> **Related:** [03 Shapes](03_Shapes_Silhouettes.md) · [04 Linework](04_Linework_Ink.md) · [05 Lighting](05_Lighting_Shading.md)

Focal points, visual weight, eye flow, balance, and negative space — how an illustration is
*arranged* so the eye reads it correctly.

## Negative space is an active design element

- Roughly **60–70% open space, 30–40% illustrated detail.**
- Do not fill every area with texture. Large areas of untouched paper create readability.

## Line rhythm & eye flow

Establish rhythm through alternating heavy and light regions; avoid uniform visual density:

> heavy contour → open space → secondary contour → fine detail → open paper → heavy contour

The eye should travel naturally through the illustration.

## Value grouping (readability at small size)

Simplify every illustration into approximately three large, readable masses:

- Large light mass
- Large midtone mass
- Large shadow mass

Group shadows together whenever possible; avoid scattered dark spots. (See
[05 Lighting](05_Lighting_Shading.md) for the shadow mechanics.)

## Contrast hierarchy — everything cannot be equally important

Reserve the highest contrast for focal points. Do not maximize contrast everywhere:

1. Primary interaction element
2. Main silhouette
3. Large structural forms
4. Secondary details
5. Decorative elements
6. Background textures

## Value hierarchy (how emphasis is established)

Emphasis order — **never establish emphasis using saturation alone:**

1. Silhouette contrast
2. Line weight
3. Value contrast
4. Shape scale
5. Detail density
6. Hue variation
7. Saturation
