# 04 · Linework & Ink

> **Tier:** ★ Most important · **Status:** Migrated (from `UI-new`, `UI-Graphic-Design`)
> **Related:** [03 Shapes](03_Shapes_Silhouettes.md) · [05 Lighting](05_Lighting_Shading.md) · [07 Materials](07_Materials_Textures.md)

**The line work defines the visual identity of the entire application.** If the line work is
wrong, the illustration fails regardless of color quality. Every visible stroke emulates a
flexible steel dip pen. **Uniform stroke widths are prohibited.**

## Stroke pressure profile

Every stroke follows a predictable pressure profile:

> medium pressure → gradually increasing → **maximum thickness near the structural center** →
> gradually releasing → fine tapered point

Abrupt width transitions are prohibited. Every stroke should feel continuous.

## Stroke weight hierarchy — three levels, always

### Primary contours — define the silhouette
Thickest stroke, darkest value, highest contrast, greatest authority. Account for ~**60–70%**
of the illustration's visual contrast.

- **Increase** weight at: bottom edges, structural supports, tree bases, stone foundations,
  root junctions, intersections, compression areas, overlaps, occluded forms, shadow-facing edges.
- **Decrease** weight at: upper edges, tips, leaves, decorative curls, branch ends, exposed
  edges, light-facing surfaces.

### Secondary contours — separate major forms
~**40–60%** of silhouette thickness. Explain volume rather than define objects. Examples:
branch connections, bark folds, stone layers, wooden planks, building joints.

### Detail lines — describe material
The lightest strokes. Examples: wood grain, bark texture, leaf veins, cracks, engraving,
fabric folds. **Every detail line must communicate at least one of:** material, curvature,
age, wear, lighting, construction. *If a line does not improve readability, it should not exist.*

## Stroke characteristics — controlled natural variation

Required: pressure variation, curvature drift, slight asymmetry, localized wobble, ink pooling,
minor overshoot, rounded transitions, soft tapering, imperfect joins.

These imperfections must appear **intentional and physically believable**. Random noise or
artificial jitter must never be used. Avoid perfectly straight lines wherever decorative —
even long borders contain subtle hand-drawn movement.

Ink is a **warm dark brown or charcoal black**, never pure digital black.

## Ink & print characteristics

Design as though drawn with archival ink on fine drawing paper. Restrained characteristics:
slight ink bleed into paper, tiny edge irregularities, mild texture within fills, soft
feathering where ink meets fiber, occasional broken segments from dry strokes, delicate
engraving hatch marks, fine cross-hatching for shadows. Capture warmth and authenticity —
not damage or distress.

## Cross-hatching — describes volume, never just darkening

- Follow surface curvature.
- Compress hatch spacing in shadow; expand toward light.
- Maximum of three intersecting hatch directions.
- Never hatch across unrelated forms.

## Line economy

Negative space is active (see [02 Composition](02_Composition_Hierarchy.md)): ~60–70% open
paper. Do not attempt to fill every area with line.
